These numbers are not unusual. After scanning hundreds of WordPress sites, we found that the average unoptimized WordPress site scores SEO 52, AEO 28, and GEO 13. That is a combined 93 out of 300 — less than a third of what is possible.

What is typically wrong? Almost everything that matters for modern search visibility:

  • No meta description — WordPress does not generate meta descriptions by default. Google shows a random snippet from your page instead of a controlled, keyword-rich description. This alone costs you 12–15 SEO points.
  • No schema markup — No Product schema, no BlogPosting schema, no Organization schema, no BreadcrumbList. Search engines see unstructured text instead of rich, typed data. Missing schema costs you 15–20 points.
  • No Open Graph tags — When someone shares your page on social media, it shows a generic preview with no image, no title control, and no description. Missing OG costs 8–10 points.
  • Zero AEO optimization — No FAQ sections, no summary blocks, no structured Q&A content, no speakable markup. AI answer engines like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews cannot find citable content on your page.
  • Zero GEO optimization — No expert quotes, no statistical data, no comparison tables, no citations. Generative search engines have nothing to pull into their AI-generated results.
  • Missing security headers — No HSTS, no X-Content-Type-Options, no X-Frame-Options. These are both security and SEO audit factors.

These are all small, missing pieces. None of them require a site redesign. None require changing your content strategy. They are technical gaps with clear, known solutions. And these small missing pieces cost you 90% of your potential visibility.

Phase 4: Fine-Tuning (Minutes 8–15)

After auto-fix and AI content enhancement, the site was already performing dramatically better. But there are always a few manual tweaks that push the score from "great" to "near-perfect." These are the optimizations that require human judgment:

  • Title tag optimization — Adjusting the title length to 50–60 characters, front-loading the primary keyword, and ensuring the title matches search intent. The auto-fix generates a good title, but a human can make it great. Impact: +3–5 points.
  • Keyword placement refinement — Ensuring the primary keyword appears in the first paragraph, in at least one H2 heading, and in the meta description. The AI content is keyword-aware, but manual positioning adds precision. Impact: +2–4 points.
  • Image alt text refinement — The auto-fix adds descriptive alt text, but you can optimize it further with specific keyword-rich descriptions that match your SEO strategy. Impact: +1–3 points.
  • Internal linking — Adding links to related content on your site. Strong internal linking improves both SEO and the depth signal that AI engines use for authority assessment. Impact: +2–5 points.
  • Content length verification — Ensuring the main content area has sufficient depth (minimum 800 words for product pages, 1500+ for blog posts). Impact: +2–3 points.

What is the difference between SEO, AEO, and GEO scores?

SEO score measures traditional search engine optimization: meta tags, headings, schema markup, technical factors, and security. AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) measures how well your content can be cited by AI answer engines like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews. GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) measures visibility in AI-generated search results. Together they give you a combined score out of 300 that represents your total search visibility across traditional and AI-powered search.
